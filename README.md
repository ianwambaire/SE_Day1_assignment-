3)List and briefly explain the phases of the Software Development Life Cycle.
 
  - Requirements: Gathering and documenting user needs and system requirements.
  - Design: Creating high-level and detailed designs of the software architecture and user interface.
  - Implementation: Writing code and building the software according to the design specifications.
  - Testing: Conducting various tests to ensure the software meets quality standards and functional requirements.
  - Deployment: Releasing the software to users or customers.
  - Maintenance: Providing ongoing support, updates, and enhancements to the software after deployment.


4)Compare and contrast the Waterfall and Agile methodologies. Provide examples of scenarios where each would be appropriate.

Comparison of Waterfall and Agile Methodologies

    approach:
        Waterfall: A linear, sequential process where one phase (e.g., requirements, design, implementation) must be completed before the next begins.
        Agile: An iterative and incremental process where development and testing occur continuously in cycles.

    Flexibility:
        Waterfall: Low flexibility; changes are challenging and costly once development begins.
        Agile: Highly flexible; adapts easily to evolving requirements.

    Phases:
        Waterfall: Phases are distinct and follow a strict order.
        Agile: Phases overlap, with iterative cycles of planning, development, and testing.

    User Involvement:
        Waterfall: Limited to the beginning (requirements) and end (testing).
        Agile: Frequent and continuous user involvement throughout the process.

    Delivery:
        Waterfall: The complete product is delivered at the end of the project.
        Agile: Functional increments of the product are delivered regularly.

    Risk Management:
        Waterfall: Higher risk of discovering problems late in the process.
        Agile: Risks are identified and addressed early through continuous feedback.

    Documentation:
        Waterfall: Extensive documentation is prepared upfront.
        Agile: Minimal documentation; prioritizes working software.

Examples of Appropriate Use

    Waterfall:
        Scenario: A payroll system with fixed regulatory requirements where all specifications are known in advance.
        Scenario: Developing software for a medical device requiring strict compliance and extensive documentation.

    Agile:
        Scenario: A mobile app where features evolve based on user feedback.
        Scenario: A dynamic e-commerce platform needing continuous updates and feature releases.
  contrast

    Waterfall is rigid, predictable, and documentation-heavy, making it ideal for stable projects with minimal changes.
    Agile is flexible, adaptive, and collaborative, making it ideal for dynamic projects requiring frequent updates and stakeholder input.

7)What are some common challenges faced by software engineers? Provide strategies to overcome these challenges.
  - Changing Requirements: Requirements may change during the development cycle, leading to scope creep and project delays.
  - Tight Deadlines: Pressure to deliver software products on schedule can result in rushed development and compromised quality.
  - Technical Debt: Accrued from shortcuts or suboptimal solutions, technical debt can impede future development efforts and increase maintenance costs.
Strategies for Overcoming Challenges: Strategies for overcoming challenges include effective communication, agile methodologies, prioritization of tasks, and regular reassessment of project goals and timelines.

 - Unit Testing: Testing individual components or modules of software.
  - Integration Testing: Testing interactions between different components or subsystems.
  - System Testing: Testing the entire software system as a whole.
  - Acceptance Testing: Testing the software against user requirements to ensure it meets user needs

#Part 2: Introduction to AI and Prompt Engineering

1)Define prompt engineering and discuss its importance in interacting with AI models.
Prompt engineering is the practice of designing and refining prompts to optimize the output of AI models, such as language models
Importance of Prompt Engineering

    Maximizing AI Capabilities
        AI models, like GPT, rely heavily on the quality and clarity of input prompts. Well-crafted prompts help the model understand user intent and produce accurate, contextually relevant, and high-quality responses.

    Achieving Desired Outcomes
        By tailoring prompts, users can guide AI systems to generate specific types of outputs, such as summarizations, creative writing, coding solutions, or technical explanations.

    Improving Efficiency
        Effective prompts reduce trial and error, saving time and computational resources. This is particularly important in scenarios like debugging code or conducting complex data analysis.

2)Provide an example of a vague prompt and then improve it by making it clear, specific, and concise. Explain why the improved prompt is more effective.
"Tell me about technology."

    Why it's vague:
        Lacks specificity: "Technology" is a broad term encompassing countless topics (e.g., AI, hardware, software, history).
        Unclear intent: The user’s purpose or focus isn't defined, leaving the AI guessing
  Improved Prompt

"Explain the impact of artificial intelligence on healthcare in the past five years."

    Why it's effective:
        Specific Topic: Focuses on artificial intelligence and its influence in a particular domain (healthcare).
        Defined Scope: Restricts the response to the past five years, ensuring relevance and manageability.
        Clear Intent: Clarifies the user’s goal—to understand the implications of AI in healthcare.
